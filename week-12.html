PHASE III · MODULE 19

Future Horizons

Where AI in medicine is heading—and how to stay current as the field evolves.

3 readings
Core Question

What does it mean to be a clinician in an era of increasingly capable AI—and how do you want to shape that future?

Overview

This final module looks ahead to the evolving landscape of clinical AI. These discussions are necessarily speculative, but the purpose isn't prediction—it's developing frameworks for thinking about rapid change.

You've now worked through foundations (how AI systems work and fail), generative AI (LLMs and their supervision), and governance (liability, institutions, patient communication). This module asks: what's next?

Key Topics

Autonomous AI Systems

Current AI mostly advises; humans decide. What happens when AI systems can act autonomously—adjusting medication doses, ordering tests, even performing procedures? What level of autonomy is appropriate, and who decides?

Consider a spectrum: advisory (AI suggests, human acts), conditional (AI acts unless human intervenes), supervised (AI acts with human monitoring), and full autonomy (AI acts independently). Different clinical contexts may warrant different points on this spectrum.

AGI and Medical Practice

"Artificial General Intelligence"—AI that matches or exceeds human capabilities across domains—remains speculative but is taken seriously by AI researchers. If AGI arrives, what happens to clinical medicine? Does the physician role fundamentally change, or is there irreducible human value in care?

The Skills of the AI-Era Clinician

If AI handles more cognitive tasks, what skills become more or less valuable? Some possibilities:

  • More valuable: AI supervision, patient relationship building, ethical reasoning, complex judgment under uncertainty, communication
  • Potentially less valued: Memorization, routine pattern recognition, documentation labor
  • New skills: Prompt engineering, AI output evaluation, algorithmic auditing
Guide Synthesis

You began this guide by using AI to transform guidelines into decision aids. You end it with frameworks for evaluating, supervising, and governing AI systems. The goal was never to make you an AI expert, but an adaptive practitioner who can navigate an AI-augmented clinical environment with confidence and appropriate skepticism.

Reflection Questions

  2. If AI becomes more capable than physicians at diagnosis, what value does the physician still provide?
  3. What's one thing you will do differently in your practice based on what you've learned in this guide?
