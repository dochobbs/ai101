Overview

Key Concepts

Data: More Than Volume

Healthcare generates enormous data volumes, but quantity alone doesn't produce good AI. What matters is data quality, representativeness, and labeling accuracy. An algorithm trained on data from one health system may fail when deployed at anotherâ€”not because the algorithm is broken, but because the underlying data distributions differ.

Key questions to ask: Where did the training data come from? Does it reflect the patient population I serve? Who labeled the outcomes, and how?

Algorithms: Pattern Extraction

Algorithms are mathematical procedures that extract patterns from data. "Machine learning" refers to algorithms that improve with exposure to more data, rather than following fixed rules written by programmers. "Deep learning" uses layered neural networks to detect increasingly abstract patterns.

The clinical analogy: just as an experienced radiologist develops pattern recognition through thousands of case exposures, ML algorithms develop statistical pattern recognition through training examples.

Compute: The Invisible Enabler

Modern AIâ€”especially large language modelsâ€”requires enormous computational resources. This has implications for where AI can run (cloud vs. local), how quickly it responds, and who controls the infrastructure. Understanding compute helps you evaluate practical deployment questions: Can this run in real-time? Does patient data leave our network?
