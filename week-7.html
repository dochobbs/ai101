PHASE II 路 MODULE 14

Centaurs and Cyborgs

Two cognitive architectures for human-AI collaboration, deskilling risks, and building adaptive practice.

4 readings
Core Question

How should clinicians think about dividing cognitive labor with AIand what are the risks of getting this wrong?

Overview

The terms "centaur" and "cyborg" come from chess, where human-AI teams competed after computers surpassed individual humans. Centaurs maintain a clear division of laborhuman does strategy, computer does calculation. Cyborgs integrate more seamlessly, with the boundary between human and machine cognition becoming blurred.

Both models have clinical applications. A centaur approach might mean using AI to generate differential diagnoses while the physician makes final decisions. A cyborg approach might mean using AI-assisted documentation that shapes how the physician thinks about the case in real-time. This week explores the trade-offs.

Key Concepts

Adaptive AI Practice

The goal isn't to pick one mode and stick with itit's to develop the judgment to shift between centaur and cyborg modes depending on context. Routine tasks may benefit from seamless integration; high-stakes decisions may require clearer separation and deliberate oversight.

This requires metacognition: awareness of your own cognitive process and when AI assistance is helping versus when it might be introducing bias or premature closure.

The Deskilling Risk

If clinicians offload cognitive tasks to AI, will they lose the underlying skills? The concern isn't hypotheticalstudies of GPS navigation show measurable impacts on spatial reasoning. In medicine, if trainees never practice differential diagnosis without AI assistance, will they develop the pattern recognition needed to catch AI errors?

Counter-arguments exist: calculators didn't destroy mathematical reasoning, and AI might free cognitive resources for higher-order clinical thinking. The truth likely depends on how AI is integrated into training and practice.

Trust Calibration

Optimal AI use requires calibrated trustneither blind acceptance nor reflexive rejection. Clinicians need to develop intuitions about when AI is likely reliable and when to be skeptical. This is a learned skill, built through experience with AI successes and failures.

The Adaptive Practitioner

"The goal of AI literacy is not to make you a machine learning expert. It's to make you an adaptive practitionersomeone who can fluidly shift between integrating AI assistance and stepping back to exercise independent clinical judgment."

Discussion Questions

  1. Think of a clinical task you do regularly. Would you approach it as a "centaur" (clear human-AI division) or "cyborg" (seamless integration)? Why?
  2. How should medical education adapt if trainees will use AI throughout their careers? Should we require "AI-free" training periods?
  3. A senior physician says "I've been doing this for 30 years, I don't need an AI second-guessing me." How would you respond?

Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish between centaur and cyborg modes of human-AI collaboration
  • Identify contexts where each mode is more appropriate
  • Evaluate deskilling risks and potential mitigations
  • Develop personal strategies for adaptive AI practice
