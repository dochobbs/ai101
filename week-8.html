Prompt Engineering for Clinicians

Zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought techniques. Practical prompt design for clinical workflows.

3 readings
Core Question

How do you communicate with AI systems to get useful, accurate, and appropriately-scoped clinical outputs?

Overview

"Prompt engineering" sounds technical, but it's fundamentally about clear communication. The way you frame a request to an LLM dramatically affects the quality of its response. This week develops practical prompting skills for clinical contextsnot to make you a prompt "expert," but to help you get more reliable outputs from AI tools you're already encountering.

Key Concepts

Zero-Shot, Few-Shot, and Chain-of-Thought

These terms describe different prompting strategies:

  • Zero-shot: Ask the model to perform a task with no examples ("Summarize this patient history")
  • Few-shot: Provide examples of the desired input-output format before your actual request
  • Chain-of-thought: Ask the model to reason step-by-step before giving a final answer

Chain-of-thought is particularly valuable in clinical contextsit makes the model's reasoning visible, allowing you to spot logical errors before accepting conclusions.

Structured Output Requests

Vague prompts produce vague outputs. Specifying the desired format, length, and structure dramatically improves utility. Compare:

  • Weak: "Tell me about metformin."
  • Better: "Provide a 3-paragraph summary of metformin covering: mechanism of action, common side effects, and key contraindications. Use language appropriate for a patient with high health literacy."

Role Assignment and Constraints

Assigning a role ("You are a clinical pharmacist reviewing medication interactions") and explicit constraints ("Do not recommend any off-label uses") can improve output quality. But be cautious: role assignment doesn't make the model actually become an expertit just shifts the statistical distribution of likely outputs.

Activity: Prompt Golf

You'll compete to diagnose a rare disease presentation using the fewest prompt iterations. This game develops intuitions about what information AI needs to produce useful differential diagnoses.

Practical Prompt Patterns

Pattern 1: The Structured Clinical Query

Patient: 67-year-old male with Type 2 diabetes Current medications: [list] Question: What are the key drug interactions I should monitor? Format your response as: 1. High-priority interactions (require action) 2. Moderate interactions (monitor) 3. Theoretical interactions (low clinical significance) For each, explain the mechanism briefly.

Pattern 2: The Differential Generator

Presentation: [symptoms, history, exam findings] Generate a differential diagnosis considering: - Most likely diagnoses (>10% probability) - Important not-to-miss diagnoses (dangerous if missed) - Atypical presentations of common conditions For each diagnosis, note one finding that would increase or decrease its likelihood.

Pattern 3: The Patient Education Translator

Medical concept: [condition/procedure/medication] Target audience: [literacy level, age, cultural considerations] Create patient education content that: - Uses analogies to everyday experiences - Avoids medical jargon (or defines it simply) - Includes what to expect and when to seek help - Is no longer than [length]

Discussion Questions

  1. You're using an LLM to draft a prior authorization letter. What information should you include in the prompt? What constraints?
  2. A colleague shares a "magic prompt" they claim always produces accurate medical information. What's your response?
  3. How might prompting strategies differ between using AI for yourself vs. for patient-facing content?

Learning Objectives

  • Apply zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought prompting strategies
  • Design structured prompts that specify format, constraints, and audience
  • Iterate on prompts based on output quality
  • Recognize the limitations of prompt engineering as a reliability strategy
