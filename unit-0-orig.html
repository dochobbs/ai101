Not with Theory—with Practice

Before you understand how AI works, learn to make it work for you. A practical on-ramp using tools you can apply today.

Hands-on activity
Core Question

How can I use AI to make learning materials work for me—before I understand how AI works?

Most courses on clinical AI start with definitions: machine learning, neural networks, training data. There's a place for that—and we'll get there in Module 1. But if you're a busy clinician, you probably want to know: what can I actually do with this, right now?

This module inverts the usual sequence. Instead of learning theory first and applying it later, you'll start by using an AI tool to solve a real problem you already have: keeping up with medical literature. Along the way, you'll develop intuitions about how these systems behave—intuitions that will make the theory easier to absorb.

Tool: NotebookLM

NotebookLM is a document-grounded AI assistant from Google. You upload your own sources—PDFs, articles, guidelines—and it answers questions based only on that material.

Why start here: Unlike general-purpose chatbots, NotebookLM is constrained to your uploaded content. This makes it safer for clinical learning: it can't invent facts from the open internet, only synthesize what you've given it.

Two High-Yield Use Cases

Before we complicate things with five examples and edge cases, let's nail two workflows that will immediately save you time.

1

Guideline → Decision Aid

Clinical guidelines are essential but dense. The AAP bronchiolitis guideline, for instance, runs dozens of pages. What you need at 2 AM is a one-page summary organized by decision points.

Sample Prompt
Summarize this guideline as a one-page clinical decision aid. Organize by key decision points (when to admit, when to discharge, when to use supplemental O2). Flag anything that changed from the previous version.
What you get: A structured summary that maps to your actual workflow, with change flags that highlight what's new. Trade-off: you'll still need to verify critical thresholds against the source.
2

Papers → Audio Overview

NotebookLM's "Audio Overview" feature generates a podcast-style conversation between two AI hosts discussing your uploaded sources. It's remarkably good for commute-time learning.

How to Use
1. Upload 2-3 related papers (e.g., recent GLP-1 agonist trials, or AI sepsis alert studies) 2. Click "Generate Audio Overview" 3. Listen during commute, workout, or dishes
What you get: A ~10-15 minute audio summary that captures main findings and puts them in conversation. Trade-off: the hosts will occasionally oversimplify or miss nuance—treat it as a first pass, not a final read.
The Underlying Principle

AI literacy for clinicians is not about understanding how the models work. It's about learning to direct them toward useful ends while recognizing their limits. You are the supervisor. The AI is the tool.

Critical Limitation

NotebookLM will hallucinate. It will miss nuance. It will confidently summarize things it doesn't fully understand.

Every output requires your verification. But starting with a tool where you control the sources—rather than asking ChatGPT to diagnose patients—is a safer sandbox for building intuition.

Hands-On Activity

  1. 1

    Create a notebook at notebooklm.google.com (requires Google account).

  2. 2

    Upload 2-3 sources relevant to your practice: a clinical guideline, a review article, a lecture PDF. Pick something you've been meaning to read.

  3. 3

    Ask three different types of questions:

    • A summary request ("Summarize the key recommendations")
    • A comparison ("How does source A differ from source B on X?")
    • An evidence query ("What evidence supports recommendation Y?")
  4. 4

    Generate an Audio Overview and listen to at least 5 minutes. Note one thing it got right and one thing it oversimplified.

  5. 5

    Reflect: Which output format (text summary vs. audio) worked better for you? What would you prompt differently next time?

By the End of This Module, You Can...

  • Create a NotebookLM notebook and upload clinical sources
  • Write prompts that extract summaries, comparisons, and decision aids
  • Identify at least one output that requires verification or contains oversimplification
  • Articulate the core loop: prompt → evaluate → refine

What's Next

Now that you've seen what AI can do in a constrained, document-grounded setting, Module 1 will give you the conceptual vocabulary to understand why it works—and why it sometimes doesn't. We'll introduce the AI Triad (Data, Algorithms, Compute) and map it to infrastructure you already know: EHRs, imaging systems, clinical decision support.

The goal isn't to make you a machine learning engineer. It's to give you the mental models to be a sophisticated consumer and supervisor of these systems.
